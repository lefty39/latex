\section{Introduction}
When a metal is subject to electromagnetic radiation, the electrons in the metal may be excited by incident photons to the point that they leave the metal in a phenomenon known as the \emph{photoelectric effect}, which was first explained in the first of Einstein's 1905 \textit{annus mirabilis} papers.

\subsection{Rayleigh and Jeans}
Einstein built off the results of Planck, who in turn had corrected the results of Jeans and Rayleigh.

Rayleigh and Jeans were concerned with modeling the behavior of a \textit{blackbody}, a hypothetical objected that absorbs all incident light, which they could approximate with a \textit{Jeans cube}, a hollow, metal cube with a small hole, such that the hole functions as a blackbody.
Such a cube, if heated, would give rise to standing electromagnetic waves along the cube's 3 axes, as the light emitted from the walls would reflect back onto the incoming wave with a \qty{180}{\degree} phase shift. These standing waves may described by

\begin{equation*}
    E(x,t) = 2E_0 \sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)
\end{equation*}
for a wavenumber $k=2\pi/\lambda$.

But these waves are subject to the boundary condition that $E=0$ at the walls, as any electric field there would immediately be cancelled by the movement of the charge of the wall itself. For a cube of side length $L$, this implies
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
        &sin(kL) = 0 \\
        &\implies kL = n\pi\\
        &\implies \frac{n\pi}{L} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\\
        &\implies \lambda = \frac{2L}{n} &\forall n \in \mathbb{N}   ,
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
or in terms of frequency,
\begin{equation*}
    \nu = \frac{nc}{2L}.
\end{equation*}
This result limits the frequencies of light possible, at which point it makes sense to talk about the \emph{density of states}, the number of states per frequency range,
\begin{equation*}
    \rho = 2 \frac{n}{\nu}= \frac{4L}{c},
\end{equation*}
where the factor of two accounts for the polarization of the wave.

This result is only one dimensional. It must now be generalized to three dimensions.

Consider the points of $\mathbb{N}^3 = \left\{  (n_x, n_y, n_z) \mid n_x,n_y,n_z \in \mathbb{N} \right\}$. Radiation of some frequency $\nu$ inside the cube would be decomposable into three components along each axis of the cube, subject to previous results, that is,
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r},t) = \left( \begin{matrix}
        E(x,t) \\
        E(y,t) \\
        E(z,t)
    \end{matrix} \right)
\end{equation*}
